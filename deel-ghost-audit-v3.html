01 // Customer Operations
01
Ghost 01

Support works.
Until it really matters.

Simple queries get fast, human, resolved. Complex EOR and payroll failures enter the same queue and fall through. No formal triage exists between them.

Trust Risk Churn Driver Scale Problem
What I found
  • Simple query reviews (payment status, withdrawal, contract questions): fast, positive, human responses across Trustpilot and G2.
  • Complex issue reviews tell a different story entirely. Gartner Peer Insights, verified 2025: "Support response over 24 hours. Even after reaching a human, they cannot answer my specific questions and I get referred to a specialist who takes days."
  • Product Hunt, verified: "When you have questions about errors, you cannot call anyone. Everything is guided through AI, which is actually just a Google search. Email tickets take 3 days to a month."
  • One verified account: 3 of 4 payrolls processed incorrectly. Employees paid by manual checks. Support took a month to respond. The mistake was on Deel's end. Deel still withheld payment until the incorrect invoice was paid.
"Their AI support bot can be incredibly circular; if you have a specific issue with a local bank delay, getting a real person to look at your actual transaction instead of quoting a help article can take way too long." Capterra verified review, February 2026
Why it is a real problem
  • Deel's support model was built when most issues were simple contractor payment queries. That is still most of the volume. But EOR and payroll complexity has grown with the customer base.
  • A missed payroll affecting 50 employees enters the same queue as a question about withdrawal fees. The response time is effectively identical. The stakes are not.
  • The cost of a missed payroll is not inconvenience. It is legal liability, employee trust, and in some markets, regulatory exposure. The support model does not reflect that asymmetry.
  • Overall CSAT remains high because simple queries dominate volume. The failure cases are statistically invisible in aggregate metrics. Nobody is measuring the right thing.
Why it probably has not been fixed
  • The ghost lives in a metric gap. High aggregate CSAT masks the problem. Complex failure cases are too infrequent to show up as a top-line issue without segment-level tracking.
  • Fixing it requires someone to own both the support classification and the downstream churn signal. That cross-functional mandate rarely happens without an explicit owner with the authority to enforce it.
  • There is a cost argument that has probably kept the status quo alive: routing complex cases to dedicated specialists is expensive, and the cases are not frequent enough to appear urgent.
Where I might be wrong
Deel may already have a tiered support system internally that is not visible from public reviews. The reviews I found could represent edge cases rather than systemic failure. If Deel's internal CSAT by issue type is strong, this ghost does not exist.
Diagnosis: How I would find the real root cause
  1. 01
    Segment tickets by issue type
    Pull 6 months of support tickets. Categorise: simple contractor, payroll correction, EOR compliance, billing dispute. Measure resolution time and CSAT by category separately, not in aggregate.
  2. 02
    Map the escalation path
    For complex EOR and payroll issues specifically: map first contact to resolution. How many handoffs? How many days? At which step does the case go cold?
  3. 03
    Correlate failures with churn
    Do customers who experienced a complex support failure churn at a higher rate? If yes, the cost is quantifiable and the fix becomes a retention investment with a number attached.
The fix
Week 1 to 2
Hard classifier at intake
Before the bot attempts anything, classify the issue into 3 buckets: simple (bot handles), complex operational (4hr human SLA), urgent/high-stakes (skip queue, named human in 2hrs). Decision tree on keywords and account tier. Not a product rebuild. A routing layer.
Week 3 to 4
Change the metric
Replace "tickets closed" with "issue resolved without escalation within 24hrs." One data change. Instantly reorients what the entire support function optimises for.
Week 5 to 6
Fix public response pattern
Stop sending the same booking link to every negative review. Five complaint categories, five specific response structures. The reviewer should feel someone read what they wrote.
Day 60
Measure separately by tier
Aggregate NPS will not show the problem. Tier-level CSAT and resolution time will. Track independently and give the fix a number it can survive the next planning cycle with.
Implementation friction 01
Who owns the classifier?
Building the intake classifier requires product, support ops, and engineering to agree on issue taxonomy and routing rules. In a fast-moving company, this alignment is harder than the build itself. Expect 2 to 3 weeks of stakeholder negotiation before a line of code is written.
Implementation friction 02
The metric change will look bad first
Switching to resolution rate will likely show a drop in the short term as the measurement gets honest. Leadership needs to be pre-sold on why a lower number is actually better. Without that, the change gets reversed before it has time to work.
Implementation friction 03
Specialist capacity does not exist yet
The 2-hour SLA for urgent cases requires a pool of specialists who are available and not overloaded. If that capacity does not exist, the SLA is a promise the system cannot keep. Hiring or retraining needs to happen before the SLA is announced externally.
02 // Brand and Acquisition
02
Ghost 02

Rippling owns
"deel reviews" on Google

A competitor buys Deel's brand keyword and wins the consideration phase before a sales call happens. The ads make specific claims Deel's own documentation contradicts. Deel is not running the equivalent play back.

Revenue Risk Brand Exposure Active Today
What I found
  • Google "deel reviews" in May 2026: first paid result is a Rippling comparison page. Headline: "Rippling vs Deel: See Why We're #1."
  • Google "rippling reviews": Deel appears nowhere in paid results. Deel is not running the same play back.
  • Rippling's page claims Deel does not automatically sync across HR, payroll, IT and finance, and does not have 650+ integrations.
  • I checked Deel's own documentation: bidirectional Workday sync, verified NetSuite SuiteApp, SAP SuccessFactors integration, 68+ native integrations. Several of Rippling's claims appear outdated or false.
  • Deel sued Rippling for defamation and deceptive trade practices in April 2025. The litigation appears to be creating brand paralysis: Deel is not countering the ads, likely due to legal caution, even where claims are demonstrably inaccurate.
"Automatically syncs across HR, payroll, IT, and finance: Rippling [tick] Deel [cross]" Rippling paid search ad, live on Google for "deel reviews," verified May 2026. Appears to contradict Deel's own product documentation.
Why it is a real problem
  • The consideration phase is where deals are won before the first sales call. A buyer who searches "deel reviews," lands on a Rippling comparison page first, and sees a feature checklist where Deel has crosses, walks into the demo with a Rippling frame already set.
  • Deel is losing brand consideration on the basis of claims its own documentation contradicts. The inaccuracy does not matter if buyers never see the rebuttal.
  • The lawsuit meant to protect Deel may be creating a brand paralysis that costs more in lost deals than it recovers in damages. Legal sensitivity is suppressing a marketing response that would be entirely legitimate.
"I genuinely wish my company would switch back to Deel. We recently moved to Rippling and the very first day of withdrawing my salary has been the worst experience." Trustpilot, 2025 — the counter-narrative exists but buyers are not finding it first
Why it probably has not been fixed
  • Paid search brand defence requires someone to notice the problem, size the impact in lost deals, and make the budget case across three teams simultaneously. That rarely happens without a single owner.
  • The litigation has made the brand team cautious. Even where Rippling's claims are factually wrong, the instinct is to let legal handle it rather than engage through marketing. Legal timelines and marketing timelines are completely different.
Where I might be wrong
Deel may be running defensive keyword campaigns in geos I did not check, or have a deliberate strategy of not engaging Rippling publicly as part of the litigation approach. If so, this is a known trade-off, not a ghost.
Diagnosis: How I would find the real root cause
  1. 01
    Audit the search landscape
    Map every keyword Rippling is buying against Deel's brand. Quantify monthly search volume and estimated impression share lost. 30-minute SEMrush pull. This is the number that funds the fix.
  2. 02
    Fact-check Rippling's claims
    Go through every checkbox on Rippling's comparison page against Deel's actual product docs. Flag false, outdated, and genuine gaps. This becomes the brief for legal and product simultaneously.
  3. 03
    Check with sales on closed-lost deals
    Ask the last 20 closed-lost deals: did the buyer mention Rippling before the first call? Map whether the Rippling narrative shows up in deal notes. This quantifies the revenue cost.
The fix
Week 1
Buy defensive positions
"Deel reviews," "deel vs rippling," "deel pricing" should return a Deel result first. Table stakes. The media cost is small relative to the deal value being lost every week this is not running.
Week 2 to 3
Build a real comparison page
Not a checklist of green ticks mirroring Rippling's format. An honest, documented comparison that addresses specific claims with actual product evidence. Honesty is more credible than a point-by-point counter-attack.
Week 3 to 5
Brief the sales team
Every AE gets a one-pager: which Rippling claims are false, what the accurate Deel answer is, what to send a buyer who has already seen the Rippling page. This costs one content day.
Month 2
Use the litigation constructively
If Rippling's ads contain demonstrably false claims, that is relevant to existing proceedings. Brief legal not to slow the marketing response but to create a record that serves both simultaneously.
Implementation friction 01
Legal will want to review everything
Any public content that directly references Rippling by name will need legal sign-off given the active litigation. Build this into the timeline. A comparison page that takes 2 weeks to write may take 6 weeks to clear. Design the legal review into the process, not as an afterthought.
Implementation friction 02
The honest comparison might reveal real gaps
Fact-checking Rippling's claims may reveal that some of them are actually true, or were true until recently. That changes the brief. The comparison page can only be "honest" if internal product owners confirm the accurate state of each feature before publication.
Implementation friction 03
Budget ownership is unclear
Defensive keyword campaigns sit at the intersection of performance marketing and brand. Neither team typically owns "competitor brand defence" as a budget line. Someone needs to be given explicit ownership and budget before anything can be purchased.
03 // AI and Operations
03
Ghost 03

The AI bot optimised
for the wrong metric

Deel launched an AI support agent in September 2025 and announced 50% absorption rate as the headline success metric. Absorption rate measures deflection, not resolution. The hard cases still fall through — and now the metrics look better, making the problem harder to see.

Ops Risk Metric Blindspot Post-Sept 2025
What I found
  • Deel launched its AI Support Agent in September 2025 and published a blog post announcing: "50% absorption rate, significantly higher than the industry average of 20 to 30%." The post also claimed the AI delivers "the same level of customer satisfaction as our human agents."
  • Reviews published after the launch contradict the satisfaction claim on complex issues. Capterra, February 2026: "Their AI support bot can be incredibly circular; getting a real person to look at your actual transaction instead of quoting a help article can take way too long."
  • Product Hunt, verified: "Everything is guided through AI which is actually just a Google search." No ability to reach a human directly.
  • Trustpilot: "I keep getting the exact same copy-pasted canned responses. Every time I open a chat an agent says I will merge this with your ticket and immediately closes the chat."
  • A Remotexa independent review found that users had to type "please connect me to a human agent" explicitly to escape the bot loop. Only then did the quality of help become genuinely good.
Why it is a real problem
  • Absorption rate measures how many queries the bot closes, not whether they were actually resolved. A 50% absorption rate on simple queries that did not need sophisticated help is not the same as a 50% improvement in support quality.
  • The bot has introduced a new failure mode that did not exist before: customers with urgent, complex issues now get trapped in a bot loop before they can reach a human. This adds friction specifically where speed matters most.
  • Worse, the aggregate metrics now look better. Overall support volume to human agents is down. But the cases that drive churn — the ones that actually needed a human — are not being resolved faster. They are being delayed longer.
  • Industry data confirms this pattern: support tickets often increase after AI implementation because failed chatbot interactions create more complex problems for human agents. The 50% the bot absorbs are the 50% that did not need much help. The other 50% still need humans, and now arrive more frustrated.
Why it probably has not been fixed
  • The AI agent is a recent, public, celebrated launch. Internally it is being measured as a success because absorption rate is up. The failure signal — complex cases taking longer to resolve — is not being tracked separately.
  • The team that built the AI agent and the team that handles complex EOR escalations are almost certainly different teams. Neither has visibility into the other's outcomes.
  • Fixing this requires admitting that the headline metric used to announce the product is measuring the wrong thing. That is a politically difficult conversation internally, regardless of whether it is technically correct.
Where I might be wrong
Deel may already track resolution rate and CSAT separately by issue type internally and be working on this. The public reviews may lag behind internal improvements. If the AI classifier was already designed to route complex issues directly to humans, this ghost does not exist in the way I have described it.
Diagnosis: How I would find the real root cause
  1. 01
    Pull resolution rate, not absorption rate
    What percentage of queries closed by the bot were actually resolved vs. reopened or escalated within 48 hours? That number tells you whether the bot is helping or just deferring.
  2. 02
    Measure CSAT by issue type post-bot
    Separate CSAT for queries the bot handled vs. queries that required escalation. If the bot-handled CSAT is high and the escalation CSAT is low, the bot is succeeding at the easy cases and failing at the hard ones.
  3. 03
    Map the bot-to-human handoff
    When a query escalates from bot to human, how much context does the human receive? If they start from zero, the bot is not just failing — it is actively making the human resolution worse by adding a frustration layer first.
The fix — three specific, implementable changes
Change 1 // Week 1
Replace the headline metric
Absorption rate out. "Resolved without human escalation within 24hrs" in. This is a data configuration change, not a product change. One week. It instantly changes what the entire bot is optimised for and surfaces the real failure rate.
Change 2 // Week 2 to 3
Build a hard classifier before the bot
Before the bot attempts to answer anything, classify the issue: simple query (bot handles), complex operational (route to human with 4hr SLA), urgent (skip queue entirely, named human in 2hrs). Decision tree on issue keywords and account tier. Not a bot rebuild. A routing layer on top of what already exists.
Change 3 // Week 3 to 4
Pass full context on escalation
When a query escalates from bot to human, the human receives: full conversation transcript, account tier, issue category, previous support history. The customer never repeats themselves. This is a CRM integration, not a bot rebuild. Three to four weeks.
Result
Absorption drops. Resolution rises.
Absorption rate probably falls from 50% to 30%. Resolution rate on handled cases rises. Cases that drive churn reach humans faster with full context. Net NPS impact is positive even though the headline AI metric looks worse. This is the correct trade-off.
Implementation friction 01
The metric change makes the bot "look worse"
Switching from absorption rate to resolution rate will show a lower number initially. The AI team will resist this because their product launch metric is being downgraded. Leadership needs to be pre-sold on why a lower number is actually better before the change is made, or it will be reversed.
Implementation friction 02
The classifier needs ground truth data
Building the intake classifier requires labelled training data: past tickets manually categorised into simple, complex, and urgent. If that dataset does not exist, it needs to be created before the classifier can be built. Expect 2 to 3 weeks of data work before the engineering build starts.
Implementation friction 03
Context-passing requires CRM cooperation
Passing full conversation context from bot to human requires the bot platform and the CRM to be integrated. If they are not already connected, this is an engineering project, not a configuration change. Timeline expands to 6 to 8 weeks and requires buy-in from two separate engineering teams.
04 // Other possible ghosts

These surfaced during the research but I could not verify them fully from public data alone. Worth investigating internally.

Possible ghost A
The pricing page claim vs. real total cost
Deel's pricing page says "no hidden fees." Independent analysts document a mandatory salary deposit, FX markups built into conversion rates, and country surcharges disclosed only during sales calls. Deel likely has legal cover for each line item individually. The ghost, if it exists, is whether buyers discovering the full cost post-signature churn at a measurably higher rate. That is a retention data question, not a pricing question.
Possible ghost B
The misclassification narrative still alive
A California state senator called for a labour investigation into Deel in 2023. Deel denied all allegations. The regulatory environment has shifted in Deel's favour under the 2025 DOL guidance. But Rippling's sales team and competitors continue to reference it in deal cycles. The ghost may be that the reputational residue of that episode is still being used against Deel in sales without a formal counter-narrative.
Possible ghost C
Post-acquisition integration lag
Deel has made 11 acquisitions to build its global infrastructure. Some reviews describe country modules operating in silos, forcing customers to maintain parallel systems. Rippling's comparison page exploits this specifically. I could not verify from public data whether this is a current product reality or a legacy from integrations now completed. Worth auditing the top 15 revenue countries specifically.
Possible ghost D
The IPO readiness pressure on operations
Deel is eyeing an IPO in 2026 at a $17B valuation. IPO readiness typically compresses operational bandwidth: legal, finance, and senior leadership focus on audit, compliance, and investor prep. This creates a window where internal operational problems get deprioritised in favour of looking clean externally. The ghosts that get hunted during IPO prep are financial, not operational. The operational ones accumulate quietly.
